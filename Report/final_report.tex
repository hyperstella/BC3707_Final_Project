\begin{abstract}
This project builds a dataset of authentic student--LLM tutoring dialogues in Computer Science and Mathematics learning contexts. The dataset contains approximately 100 conversations from around 20 students, with 162 labeled student--LLM interaction turns. We develop an annotation framework for learning scenarios, learning goals, prompt quality, response quality, satisfaction, and multi-turn repair behavior. Our analyses show that Concept Explanation is the dominant learning goal, prompt quality has only a weak positive relationship with response quality, and incorporating pedagogical quality scores improves automated learning goal classification performance. We also find that LLMs often repair student confusion successfully through changed explanations, step-by-step structure, and concrete examples. Overall, this project provides a real-world dataset and analysis framework for studying LLM tutoring behavior in authentic educational settings.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Large Language Models (LLMs) are increasingly used as tutoring tools, yet their effectiveness as pedagogical agents remains underexplored. Although prior work studies LLMs in educational settings, much of it relies on simulated or structured interactions that may not reflect how students naturally use these systems. Existing datasets also rarely focus on authentic college-level Computer Science and Mathematics help-seeking conversations, where tutoring is highly dynamic and often involves multi-step reasoning, clarification requests, and conceptual repair across multiple turns.

Therefore, our project builds a dataset of authentic student--LLM tutoring dialogues drawn from real problem-solving interactions. Unlike benchmark-style datasets or simulated tutoring systems, our dataset captures interactions initiated by real students asking genuine questions about coursework, assignments, exams, and projects. The dataset emphasizes pedagogical quality in addition to correctness.

The main research questions are:

\begin{itemize}
    \item How do college students naturally use LLMs in tutoring interactions, especially in CS and Math?
    \item What learning goals and scenarios appear most frequently?
    \item How does prompt quality relate to response quality?
    \item How do LLMs respond to follow-up questions and repair student confusion?
    \item Can the annotation framework we developed help models automatically predict students' learning goals and satisfaction levels?
\end{itemize}

To study these questions, we develop an annotation and evaluation framework for analyzing learning goals, tutoring quality, classification performance, and multi-turn repair behavior in student--LLM dialogues.

\section{Methods}

\subsection{Datasets}

We collected 100 student--LLM tutoring dialogues from around 20 students, primarily from Barnard College and Columbia University. The dataset focuses on CS and Math learning contexts, including programming assignments, debugging, conceptual learning, lecture review, and exam preparation.

During preprocessing, screenshot-based prompts containing code, equations, or problem statements were manually converted into text so that conversations could be stored consistently in JSON format. We also excluded dialogues with large amounts of graphs or highly visual outputs because they were difficult to represent reliably in text-based JSON and harder to annotate consistently.

The collected dialogues were first organized into Word documents and then converted into structured JSON files. During this process, we found that automatic conversion by ChatGPT sometimes summarized or abbreviated long conversations, even when prompted to preserve the full text. To reduce information loss, we refined the prompts, used Claude for some long-form conversion, and manually copied dialogue text when necessary. These steps helped preserve dialogue completeness and improve annotation consistency.

Each JSON file stores the dialogue content, model identity, annotation labels, quality scores, and evaluator notes. For example, one dialogue asks why memory must be reaccessed after a register is overwritten; it is labeled as Assignment Help with the learning goal Concept Explanation and includes separate prompt-quality and response-quality scores.

The final dataset contains 100 full conversations and 162 labeled student--LLM interaction turns, where each turn consists of one student prompt paired with one corresponding LLM response. Among these, 64 turns from 40 conversations were human-annotated by our team, while 98 turns from 60 additional conversations were labeled using ChatGPT. \subsection{Models and Approach}

Our project analyzes authentic tutoring interactions rather than training a new LLM from scratch. The dialogues were collected from student interactions with widely used general-purpose transformer-based LLM systems, including ChatGPT \citep{openai2023gpt4}, Gemini \citep{team2023gemini}, and Claude \citep{anthropic2024claude3}. The collected ChatGPT dialogues include GPT-4.5, GPT-5, and GPT-5.5 interactions in both Thinking and Instant modes; Gemini dialogues include Gemini 3 in Fast and Thinking modes; and Claude dialogues include Claude Sonnet 4.6.

These systems were selected because they are among the most commonly used AI assistants in real student learning environments, particularly for coursework, debugging, conceptual learning, and exam preparation.

For GPT-assisted annotation, we used GPT-4o-mini with few-shot prompting and manually annotated examples to label additional turns according to our annotation framework. We selected GPT-4o-mini because it provides relatively strong instruction-following performance while remaining efficient and cost-effective for large-scale annotation tasks.

\subsection{Experiments}

To analyze tutoring behavior and pedagogical quality, we developed a two-level annotation framework for the collected dialogues.

Each student prompt is first categorized according to the learning scenario:

\begin{itemize}
    \item Assignment Help
    \item Exam Preparation
    \item Lecture Learning
    \item Project or Research Support
    \item Not Clear
\end{itemize}

Each prompt is then labeled according to the learning goal:

\begin{itemize}
    \item Concept Explanation
    \item Step-by-Step Guidance
    \item Answer Clarification
    \item Direct Answer Generation
    \item Strategy or Hint Seeking
    \item Debugging / Error Fixing
\end{itemize}

To establish annotation consistency and improve annotation efficiency, 40 conversations were manually annotated first to develop annotation guidelines and identify common tutoring patterns. The remaining 60 conversations were then annotated with GPT-4o-mini using few-shot prompting examples. Learning goals and prompt/response quality scores were annotated at the turn level, where each turn consists of one student prompt paired with one corresponding LLM response. In contrast, learning scenarios were annotated at the conversation level to reflect the broader educational context of the interaction.

We additionally evaluate prompt quality and response quality on the 64 human-annotated turns. Prompt quality is scored from 1 to 5 across three dimensions:

\begin{itemize}
    \item Clarity of Intent
    \item Specificity and Precision
    \item Context Provision
\end{itemize}

Response quality is evaluated using:

\begin{itemize}
    \item Coherence
    \item Correctness
    \item Guidance Quality
\end{itemize}

The weighted response quality score is computed as:

\[
S_r = 2C + R + 3G
\]

where $C$ denotes coherence, $R$ denotes correctness, and $G$ denotes guidance quality. Guidance receives the highest weight because effective tutoring should help students build understanding and solve problems independently. Coherence is weighted second because tutoring responses must remain logically organized and easy to follow. Correctness receives the lowest weight because modern LLMs already achieve relatively high factual accuracy on many common educational tasks.

We then train classifiers for goal prediction and satisfaction prediction. Goal prediction uses the full 162-turn labeled dataset, while satisfaction prediction uses the 64 human-annotated turns. We compare three models:

\begin{itemize}
    \item TF-IDF with logistic regression
    \item Sentence-Transformer MLP
    \item TextCNN
\end{itemize}

Finally, we analyze repair behavior in the 64 follow-up exchanges drawn from 33 multi-turn conversations. Each exchange is labeled for repair outcome and repair behavior to study how LLMs respond to students' clarification and confusion.

\subsection{Prompt and Response Quality}

We analyzed the relationship between prompt quality and response quality using the 64 human-annotated turns collected from 40 conversations. We focused specifically on human annotations because they are more likely to reflect reliable pedagogical judgments than GPT-generated annotations, which may introduce additional noise or systematic bias. During our preliminary experiments, we observed that GPT-based annotators occasionally assigned disproportionately high scores to LLM responses, suggesting a potential self-evaluation bias in automated scoring.

Across the 64 human-annotated turns, the mean normalized prompt quality score is $0.757$ ($\sigma = 0.216$), while the mean normalized response quality score is $0.810$ ($\sigma = 0.192$). Both averages are relatively high, suggesting that modern LLMs generally produce coherent and pedagogically effective responses even when student prompts are only moderately detailed or well-specified. The slightly higher average response quality further suggests that LLMs are often able to compensate for incomplete or underspecified prompts.

The Pearson correlation between prompt quality and response quality is $r = 0.228$ ($p = 0.070$), indicating a weak positive relationship between the two variables. Although higher-quality prompts are associated with marginally stronger responses, the correlation remains relatively small, suggesting that response quality is only loosely dependent on prompt specificity and clarity. This finding may reflect the robustness of modern LLMs across a broad range of prompt quality levels. Figure~\ref{fig:pq-rq-scatter} visualizes this relationship.

\subsection{Goal and Satisfaction Classification}

To evaluate whether our annotation framework can support automated pedagogical intent detection, we trained three classifiers on the goal prediction and satisfaction prediction tasks: (1) TF-IDF with logistic regression, combining bag-of-ngrams features with normalized quality scores; (2) a Sentence-Transformer MLP using \texttt{all-MiniLM-L6-v2} embeddings concatenated with quality scores; and (3) a TextCNN with learned token embeddings.

All experiments were conducted at the turn level rather than the conversation level. Initial experiments using only the 64 human-annotated turns produced goal prediction accuracy of approximately 0.50 on a 6-sample test set. However, this result was too unstable to interpret reliably, since a single misclassification changes accuracy by 16.7 percentage points.

To increase the amount of labeled training data, we applied GPT-4o-mini few-shot annotation to previously unlabeled turns from the remaining conversations. Using the manually annotated examples as demonstrations, GPT-4o-mini generated additional labels following the established annotation framework. This process expanded the dataset from 64 human-annotated turns to 162 labeled turns in total, enabling a more reliable 8:1:1 train-validation-test split with 17 held-out test samples and substantially more training data.

For goal prediction, the 162-turn dataset was split 8:1:1 stratified by class (128 train, 17 validation, 17 test). Both the training and test sets contain a mixture of human-annotated and GPT-labeled examples. For satisfaction prediction, leave-one-out cross-validation (LOOCV) was performed on the 64 human-annotated turns. Results are summarized in Table~\ref{tab:clf-results}.

For goal prediction, both TF-IDF and Sentence-MLP achieve 70.6\% accuracy, outperforming the 58.8\% majority baseline by 11.8 percentage points. A particularly important finding is the contribution of pedagogical quality features. Incorporating coherence, correctness, and guidance scores substantially improved classification performance across both TF-IDF and Sentence-MLP models. Before adding quality-score features, TF-IDF achieved a macro-F1 of 0.424 and Sentence-MLP achieved 0.465. After incorporating the quality scores, macro-F1 increased to 0.531 for TF-IDF ($+0.107$) and 0.524 for Sentence-MLP ($+0.059$).

These improvements suggest that pedagogical quality signals provide meaningful discriminative information beyond surface-level textual features alone. In particular, the quality scores appear especially useful for distinguishing minority classes such as Debugging/Error Fixing and Step-by-Step Guidance, which are more difficult to separate using text features only. TF-IDF achieves the strongest overall macro-F1 (0.531) and correctly classifies Debugging/Error Fixing with perfect precision and recall. In contrast, TextCNN, which relies only on text features and cannot leverage explicit pedagogical quality scores, drops to 58.8\% accuracy and a macro-F1 of 0.217, collapsing largely to the majority class. This further supports the value of incorporating pedagogical evaluation signals into downstream educational NLP tasks.

For satisfaction prediction, TF-IDF with logistic regression again achieves the strongest overall performance, with 57.8\% accuracy and a macro-F1 of 0.525, both slightly above the 54.7\% majority baseline. The stronger performance of TF-IDF relative to neural models is expected given the limited dataset size: sparse n-gram features generalize more effectively from small datasets than learned neural representations, which typically require substantially more training data. Although TextCNN achieves moderate accuracy, its low macro-F1 (0.298) indicates substantial class imbalance effects, as the model predominantly predicts the neutral majority class rather than learning balanced decision boundaries across all categories.

\subsection{Repair Outcome Distribution and LLM Repair Behavior}

To study how LLMs handle student confusion across multiple turns, we analyzed all 64 follow-up exchanges (turn\,id\,$\geq$\,2) drawn from the 33 multi-turn conversations in our dataset. For each exchange, GPT-4o-mini classified (1) a single \emph{repair outcome} and (2) zero or more \emph{repair behaviors} from a predefined taxonomy.

\paragraph{Repair outcome} was classified into one of four categories: \emph{Successful Repair} (the LLM's follow-up response clearly resolves the confusion), \emph{Partial Repair} (confusion is addressed but gaps remain), \emph{Failed Repair} (the LLM repeats itself without meaningfully helping), or \emph{Repeated Misconception} (the LLM reinforces an error).

\paragraph{Repair behavior} was tagged with any applicable subset of six strategies: Changed strategy after follow-up; Used simplification; Used step-by-step structure; Used analogy or visual explanation; Used concrete example; Student showed further confusion afterward.

\subsection{Limitations of the Classification}

The goal classification experiments use a mixture of human-annotated and GPT-labeled turns, since the 8:1:1 split was applied to the full 162-turn dataset. As a result, evaluation is conducted partly against silver-standard rather than fully human-validated labels. In contrast, satisfaction prediction uses only the 40 human-annotated annotations through LOOCV, making that evaluation fully grounded in human judgement. Additionally, minority classes such as Debugging/Error Fixing and Direct Answer Generation remain underrepresented, limiting per-class classification performance.

\section{Related Work}

Prior work on LLM tutoring spans several strands, including tutoring dialogue datasets, analyses of real student–AI interactions, and methods for improving tutor behavior from dialogue data. First, tutoring research has long emphasized that effective learning support depends on multi-turn interaction, scaffolding, and feedback rather than answer delivery alone. More recently, this perspective has been extended to LLM-based tutoring through datasets and evaluation frameworks that explicitly model pedagogical dialogue \citep{macina2023mathdial, sharma2026convolearn}.

A growing line of work focuses on \textbf{tutoring-oriented dialogue datasets}. MathDial introduced a large tutoring dataset grounded in math reasoning problems and annotated with rich pedagogical properties, showing that such data can be used to fine-tune models to act more like tutors than solvers \citep{macina2023mathdial}. ConvoLearn similarly frames tutoring through explicit pedagogical dimensions such as cognitive engagement, formative assessment, and metacognition, though it is semi-synthetic rather than based on naturally occurring student use \citep{sharma2026convolearn}. These datasets are relevant because they show how tutoring behavior can be operationalized and modeled, but they differ from our project in that we focus on authentic student--LLM interactions.

A second line of work studies \textbf{real student--AI interactions}. StudyChat analyzes real student dialogues with ChatGPT in an AI course, providing evidence about how students actually ask questions and follow up on model responses \citep{mcnichols2025studychat}. Related work has also examined AI tutor use in real courses at scale: Russell et al. study interaction patterns with an AI tutor in a large introductory STEM course and relate these patterns to course performance \citep{russell2025unlocking}. Beyond STEM tutoring, Han et al. analyze student--ChatGPT dialogue in EFL writing education, showing that authentic student--AI interactions can also reveal intent patterns and satisfaction signals in writing-support contexts \citep{han2023exploring}. Together, these works broaden the landscape from controlled tutoring setups to real educational use.

A third line of work asks how dialogue data can be used to \textbf{improve tutors or model learning}. Scarlatos et al. train LLM-based tutors to maximize downstream student correctness while maintaining pedagogical quality, showing that dialogue-level optimization can improve tutor responses \citep{scarlatos2025training}. In a related direction, Scarlatos et al. also show that tutor--student dialogues can support knowledge tracing, using LLMs to identify skills and student correctness across turns and then modeling student understanding over time \citep{scarlatos2025exploring}. These papers are important for our project because they suggest that tutoring dialogues are useful not only for descriptive analysis, but also for downstream tutor improvement and student modeling.

Finally, recent work has begun to examine \textbf{engagement and risks}. Bai et al. study GPTutor, a generative-AI-powered tutoring system, and analyze behavioral, cognitive, and emotional engagement in higher education \citep{bai2025promoting}. At the same time, Bastani et al. show that generative AI without sufficient guardrails can harm learning, as students may use the system as a crutch and perform worse once support is removed \citep{bastani2025generative}. These findings motivate our interest not only in whether LLM tutors are helpful, but in what kinds of feedback and interaction patterns are pedagogically productive.

Building on these strands of work, our project focuses specifically on authentic, open-ended student--LLM tutoring dialogues, with fine-grained annotation of tutor feedback and student follow-up behavior. Compared with prior work, our goal is to connect real usage data with pedagogical analysis and scalable annotation through few-shot prompting.

\vspace{-1em}

\section{Conclusion}

In this project, we build a dataset of authentic student--LLM tutoring dialogues focused on Computer Science and Mathematics learning contexts. Motivated by the growing use of LLMs as informal educational tools, our project studies how students naturally interact with these systems and whether LLM responses provide meaningful pedagogical support beyond simply generating correct answers.

Our analyses reveal several notable findings. Concept Explanation dominates student learning goals (59\%), and Lecture Learning is the most common scenario (46\%), suggesting that students primarily use LLMs to strengthen conceptual understanding rather than request direct answers. Prompt quality and response quality show only a weak positive correlation ($r = 0.228$), indicating that modern LLMs are relatively robust to underspecified prompts. In classification experiments, TF-IDF and Sentence-MLP outperform the majority baseline for goal prediction, and incorporating pedagogical quality scores improves macro-F1, highlighting the value of pedagogical evaluation signals beyond surface-level text features alone. Our repair analysis further shows that LLMs successfully resolve student confusion in most follow-up exchanges, primarily by changing explanation strategies, applying step-by-step structure, and providing concrete examples.

\subsection{Limitations}

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Dataset size and class imbalance.} The dataset contains only 162 labeled turns, including 64 human-annotated turns, limiting classifier reliability and reducing performance on minority categories such as Debugging/Error Fixing and Direct Answer Generation. The relatively small test set also makes some evaluation metrics sensitive to individual predictions.

    \item \textbf{Silver-standard annotation quality.} Most labels were generated using GPT-4o-mini few-shot prompting rather than human annotation. Preliminary experiments suggest that GPT-based annotators may assign inflated scores to LLM responses, meaning some evaluations rely partly on silver-standard rather than fully human-validated labels.

    \item \textbf{Annotation subjectivity.} Dimensions such as Guidance Quality and pedagogical usefulness involve subjective judgement, and the project does not currently measure inter-annotator agreement across annotators.

    \item \textbf{Limited population and linguistic diversity.} The dataset consists only of English-language dialogues collected from students at Columbia University and Barnard College, limiting broader generalizability across institutions, disciplines, languages, and cultural learning contexts.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Future Work}

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Dataset expansion.} Future work should collect dialogues from more institutions, disciplines, languages, and student populations while substantially increasing the number of human-annotated examples.

    \item \textbf{Improved annotation quality.} Future work should establish formal inter-annotator agreement protocols and reduce reliance on GPT-generated silver annotations by incorporating multiple human annotators and adjudication procedures.

    \item \textbf{Stronger classification models.} Larger datasets would enable the use of more expressive transformer-based models and multi-task learning approaches across goal prediction, satisfaction prediction, and repair outcome classification.

    \item \textbf{Improving tutoring behavior.} Future research could use the annotation framework developed here to fine-tune or prompt LLMs toward more pedagogically effective tutoring strategies that better support independent problem-solving and conceptual understanding.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Broader Impact}

Overall, this project contributes a real-world dataset and pedagogically grounded analysis framework for studying LLM tutoring behavior in authentic educational settings. As LLMs become increasingly integrated into student learning workflows, understanding how they support conceptual understanding and multi-turn learning interactions becomes increasingly important. The annotation framework, quality scoring rubric, and repair behavior taxonomy developed in this work provide a foundation for future research on more effective, reliable, and student-centered AI tutoring systems.
